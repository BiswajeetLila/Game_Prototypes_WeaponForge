# 3. Core Thesis / Idea

**Core idea (player-voice, ~100 words):**

You drop into a ruined outbreak zone with a weak gun, a few teammates, and zombies already clawing through the walls. Every kill gives us points, and every point is a choice: buy ammo, open a door, repair the safehouse, upgrade a weapon, or stabilize a containment breach before the room mutates. The map keeps getting worse if we ignore it, and rival survivor squads are also moving through the area taking supplies or triggering trouble. A good run feels like we are barely holding the disaster together while getting stronger just fast enough to survive one more round.

# 4. Gameplay Journey

**Detailed D1 First 60 seconds:**

- 0-5s: The player sees a first-person view inside a sealed military quarantine lobby. Emergency lights flash, a teammate marker appears, and a simple objective says "Survive Round 1." A short camera nudge highlights a barricaded window shaking under zombie pressure.
- 5-15s: The first zombie breaks through. The player drags the right thumb to aim and taps fire. Hit markers, blood impact, recoil, and a points popup instantly confirm the shot. A second prompt teaches reload after the magazine runs low.
- 15-25s: The first feedback loop closes. The zombie dies, points are added, and a nearby wall weapon glows. The player has enough points to understand that killing zombies creates buying power.
- 25-40s: A second window breaks and two zombies enter from different angles. The player moves with the left thumb, shoots while backing up, and taps a repair prompt to rebuild a barricade. The room feels readable but no longer safe.
- 40-60s: The round ends and the player sees three exciting choices at once: buy a stronger weapon, open the next door, or spend resources on the safehouse. A containment breach warning pulses behind the locked door, hinting that the map itself will change if the team waits too long.

# 5. Hypothesis of Why This Would Work

Mobile shooters often deliver strong aiming, weapon collection, and short-session competition, but they rarely create the cooperative survival pressure and map mastery that makes zombie horde modes memorable. This concept bets that mobile players will respond to a more dynamic version of round-based zombies if the core touch controls feel immediate and the match gives them meaningful choices every minute: survive the wave, open the map, upgrade the team safehouse, manage containment breaches, and react to rival squads.

The strongest bet is that dynamic map pressure makes a familiar zombie loop feel new. Round-based survival already works because it is easy to understand and endlessly replayable; games such as Call of Duty Zombies, Left 4 Dead, and Killing Floor show the appeal of co-op undead pressure. This concept adds mobile-readable strategic variety through containment breaches, a buildable safehouse, and rival survivor squads so each run produces a different story instead of only a higher enemy count.

# 6. Risks

**Single fragile assumption:**

Mobile players will accept a deeper, more tactical zombie survival loop if the touch controls, onboarding, and first-session pacing make shooting, buying, building, and breach management feel fast rather than overwhelming.

# 7. Reference Games

1. Call of Duty: Zombies - Treyarch / Activision, 2008-present, console / PC / mobile.
   - Why it's comparable: It defines round-based zombie survival with points, perks, doors, weapon upgrades, and co-op escalation.
   - What we share: The survival structure, escalating rounds, map unlocks, power fantasy, weapon progression, and team revive pressure.
   - What we don't: Outbreak Protocol adds dynamic containment breaches, a buildable safehouse, rival survivor squads, and a mobile-first control and session structure.

2. Left 4 Dead 2 - Valve, 2009, PC / console.
   - Why it's comparable: It proves the appeal of co-op zombie pressure, special infected roles, and dramatic rescue moments.
   - What we share: Team survival, readable special enemies, panic moments, and the need to protect allies under pressure.
   - What we don't: Left 4 Dead 2 is campaign-driven, while Outbreak Protocol uses compact round-based maps with persistent in-match economy and upgrade choices.

3. Killing Floor 2 - Tripwire Interactive, 2016, PC / console.
   - Why it's comparable: It centers on wave survival, weapon economy, enemy composition, and increasingly punishing horde pressure.
   - What we share: Wave escalation, squad coordination, weapon buying, special enemies, and late-round intensity.
   - What we don't: Killing Floor 2 does not focus on map mutation, defensive safehouse construction, or rival squads competing for resources inside the same run.

**Genre mashup formula:**

Round-based zombies x mobile FPS x light base defense x dynamic outbreak events.

# 8. Progression

**Rest of D1 (~5-10 min after first 60s):**

- The player completes the first few rounds and learns that kills create points.
- The player buys a wall weapon and feels the immediate jump in power.
- The player opens the first door and discovers a larger room with more zombie entry points.
- The player sees the first containment breach warning and chooses whether to stabilize it or keep spending on weapons.
- The player reaches the safehouse and installs the first upgrade, such as reinforced doors or an ammo station.
- A rival survivor squad appears, takes a supply crate, and forces the player to decide whether to chase, ignore, or help them.
- The player restores power, unlocking perks and stronger map utilities.
- The player is eventually overwhelmed or extracts after a short objective, then sees match rewards, account XP, weapon XP, and a clear next unlock.

**Vague D1-D14 idea:**

| Day | What the player has | What brings them back |
| --- | --- | --- |
| D1 | The player understands movement, shooting, points, doors, basic safehouse upgrades, and the threat of containment breaches. | The first weapon unlock, a better high-round score, and curiosity about what happens if a breach is stabilized or ignored. |
| D3 | The player has several weapons, early attachments, one or two perk variants, and enough map knowledge to plan routes. | Daily challenges push them to try different weapons, safehouse builds, and breach strategies with friends or matchmaking squads. |
| D7 | The player has a preferred loadout, understands rival squad behavior, and can reach mid-round escalation consistently. | Weekly objectives, rare cosmetics, map secrets, and the desire to beat a previous high round create a stronger mastery loop. |
| D14 | The player has multiple upgraded weapons, several operator cosmetics, and a clear understanding of team roles and map events. | Seasonal challenges, harder breach modifiers, extraction goals, and leaderboard-style high-round competition provide long-term goals. |

## 9.1 Synthetic Testing Materials - Design (Text)

### Full Description of Core Loop (Stage 1 required, ~125 words.)

You start in a sealed outbreak zone with a weak weapon and zombies breaking through the room. Every kill gives you points, and the end of each round gives you a short chance to spend them before the next wave hits. You can buy a stronger gun, open a new route, repair barricades, activate traps, or push deeper toward the power switch and upgrade machines. As rounds climb, zombies get faster, special infected appear, and containment breaches begin changing rooms with hazards like toxic fog, electrical surges, or locked-down doors. Your squad can also build up a safehouse with useful defenses and support stations, but defending it means choosing when to hold ground and when to run. Rival survivor squads sometimes enter the map, taking supplies or triggering events. A run is a constant choice between surviving now, investing for later, and risking one more objective for better rewards.

### Full Description of Core Loop + 1 Meta Progression (Stage 1, optional for run-based, ~165 words.)

You begin each run trapped inside a zombie outbreak site with only a basic weapon and a few safe rooms. Zombies break through windows, vents, and doors, and every kill gives you points to spend during the brief calm between rounds. You open new areas, buy weapons, repair defenses, restore power, activate traps, upgrade gear, and decide whether to stabilize containment breaches before they mutate parts of the map. The safehouse gives your squad a place to invest in defenses, ammo, healing, and escape options, but the horde can still overrun it. Rival survivor squads move through the same map, sometimes helping, sometimes stealing resources, and sometimes becoming a new threat. After each match, your weapons and account advance, giving you new guns, attachments, perks, cosmetics, and challenge goals. The next run starts familiar but stronger, with more tools and better knowledge of how to push deeper, survive longer, and handle the map's chaos.

### Store-Page Variant (Stage 1b optional, ~50 words.)

Survive endless zombie rounds in a mobile-first co-op FPS. Buy weapons, unlock the map, build your safehouse, contain dangerous breaches, and outlast rival survivor squads. Every run escalates differently as the outbreak mutates around you. Fight solo or with friends and see how long your squad can survive.

### Storefront Image Prompt

Mobile game storefront key art showing a small tactical squad with weak weapons defending a makeshift safehouse inside a ruined outbreak zone as zombies claw through breached walls, a glowing containment breach mutates the room, and rival survivors escape with supplies in the background.

### First 1-5 Minutes the Player Experiences ~270 words.

The first session opens inside a military quarantine lobby with emergency lights, cracked glass, and zombies pounding on barricaded windows. The player is given only two clear inputs at first: move with the left thumb and aim/fire with the right side of the screen. A zombie breaks through, the player shoots it, and the game immediately rewards the kill with hit markers, points, and a short sound sting. More zombies enter, teaching the player to move while shooting and reload before being surrounded.

After the first round ends, the room calms down for a few seconds. A wall weapon glows, a locked door shows its point cost, and the safehouse build node highlights a cheap first upgrade. The player buys one option and instantly sees the consequence: better firepower, a new route, or a stronger defensive position. In the second round, more zombies enter from multiple windows, forcing the player to use movement and space rather than standing still.

By the third or fourth minute, the team opens the first door. A containment breach warning appears in the next area, showing unstable gas and mutated zombies behind a sealed lab window. A rival survivor squad rushes past on the far side of the room and grabs a supply crate. The player understands the promise: this is not just shooting waves, it is surviving a map that keeps changing while other humans compete for the same resources.

### D1-D14 Player Journey / Progression Description (Stage 2 input, ~340 words.)

Day 1: The player learns the basic zombie loop: shoot, earn points, spend, survive, and repeat. The first rewards are simple and immediate, such as a new weapon, a first attachment, and a small account level increase. The player should leave the first session knowing that opening doors, buying upgrades, and improving the safehouse creates a better run.

Day 3: The player has seen several containment breach outcomes and understands that ignoring a breach changes the map in dangerous but rewarding ways. They have a few weapon choices, enough attachments to shape a favorite gun, and early perk options that support different playstyles such as faster reloads, stronger revives, or better movement. Daily challenges encourage them to try new weapons and stabilize or exploit different breach types.

Day 7: The player has a stronger sense of mastery. They can reach midgame rounds, restore power reliably, and make planned safehouse upgrades. Rival survivor squads become part of the strategy: sometimes they are worth helping for later support, and sometimes they must be beaten to resources. Weekly challenges and map secrets begin pulling the player toward repeat play.

Day 14: The player has a small arsenal of leveled weapons, preferred perks, cosmetics, and clearer team roles. The motivation shifts from learning to optimization: higher rounds, cleaner extractions, better breach management, faster objective clears, rare cosmetics, and seasonal modifiers. The game should feel like a familiar ritual that still produces unpredictable stories.
